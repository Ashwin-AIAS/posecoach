# General Coaching Principles

## Breathing and Bracing
Proper breathing is foundational to safe lifting. The Valsalva maneuver — taking a deep breath and bracing the core before a heavy rep — creates intra-abdominal pressure that stabilizes the spine. The lifter should inhale before the eccentric phase (lowering), hold and brace during the hardest portion of the lift, and exhale during or after completing the concentric phase (lifting). For lighter loads or higher rep work, rhythmic breathing (exhale on exertion, inhale on lowering) is acceptable. The key rule is to never hold the breath for multiple consecutive reps with heavy weight, as this can cause dangerous blood pressure spikes.

## Warm-Up Protocol
A proper warm-up prepares the body for the specific demands of the workout. It should include 5–10 minutes of general cardiovascular activity (walking, cycling, or rowing) to raise core temperature, followed by dynamic stretching targeting the joints and muscles involved in that day's exercises. Before performing working sets, lifters should do 2–3 progressively heavier warm-up sets of each exercise. For example, before squatting 80kg for working sets, a lifter might do sets at 20kg (empty bar), 40kg, and 60kg. Static stretching before heavy lifting is not recommended as it can temporarily reduce force production.

## Progressive Overload
Muscles adapt and grow stronger only when they are progressively challenged beyond their current capacity. This can be achieved by increasing the weight lifted, increasing the number of repetitions, increasing the number of sets, decreasing rest periods, or improving the quality of each rep (better form through fuller range of motion and more controlled tempo). For beginners, adding weight to the bar every session (linear progression) is effective. As a lifter advances, progression becomes slower and more structured through periodization — cycling through phases of higher volume (more reps) and higher intensity (heavier weight).

## Rest Periods Between Sets
Rest periods depend on the training goal. For maximal strength (1–5 reps with heavy weight), 3–5 minutes of rest between sets allows full phosphocreatine recovery. For hypertrophy (6–12 reps with moderate weight), 1–2 minutes of rest creates metabolic stress that contributes to muscle growth. For muscular endurance (15+ reps with light weight), 30–60 seconds of rest is appropriate. Cutting rest periods too short on heavy compound lifts leads to form breakdown and increased injury risk.

## Range of Motion
Full range of motion through each exercise is essential for balanced muscle development and joint health. Partial reps should only be used intentionally as an advanced technique, not as a default due to poor mobility or excessive weight. If a lifter cannot achieve the full range of motion for an exercise, the priority should be addressing the mobility limitation rather than loading more weight onto a restricted pattern.

## Tempo and Control
Every rep should be performed under control, especially the eccentric (lowering) phase. A controlled eccentric phase typically takes 2–3 seconds and is important for muscle development, joint health, and injury prevention. Dropping or bouncing the weight during the eccentric phase removes the muscle-building stimulus and increases injury risk. The concentric (lifting) phase can be performed more explosively, but always with control.

## Unilateral Training and Imbalances
Most people have strength imbalances between their left and right sides. Unilateral exercises (lunges, single-arm presses, single-leg exercises) expose and correct these imbalances. When a significant imbalance is detected — for example, if one side consistently produces lower form scores in PoseCoach — the lifter should prioritize the weaker side by starting sets with that side and matching the stronger side to the same rep count.

## When to Stop a Set
A set should be stopped when form breaks down, regardless of the target rep count. Continuing to perform reps with deteriorating form — rounded back on deadlifts, knee valgus on squats, excessive body swing on curls — trains the wrong movement pattern and increases injury risk. It is better to end a set 1–2 reps early with good form than to push through with poor form. This concept is known as "reps in reserve" (RIR), and most training programs recommend stopping with 1–3 reps in reserve for most working sets.

## Recovery and Frequency
Muscles need 48–72 hours of recovery between intense training sessions targeting the same muscle groups. Training a muscle group while it is still significantly sore or fatigued reduces performance and increases overuse injury risk. However, light movement and mobility work on rest days (active recovery) can aid recovery. Sleep is the single most important recovery factor — 7–9 hours per night is recommended for trainees.
