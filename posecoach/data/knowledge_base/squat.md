# Barbell Back Squat — Form Guide

## Exercise Overview
The barbell back squat is a compound, multi-joint lower-body exercise that primarily targets the quadriceps, glutes, and hip extensors. It is one of the most fundamental strength training movements and a staple in athletic training programs.

## Target Muscles
Primary: quadriceps (rectus femoris, vastus lateralis, vastus medialis, vastus intermedius), gluteus maximus.
Secondary: hamstrings, erector spinae, core stabilizers (transverse abdominis, obliques), adductors, calves (soleus, gastrocnemius).

## Joint Actions
- Hip: flexion during descent, extension during ascent
- Knee: flexion during descent, extension during ascent
- Ankle: dorsiflexion during descent, plantarflexion during ascent

## Key Joint Angles — Correct Form

### Knee Flexion
At the bottom of a full-depth squat, knee flexion should reach approximately 110–130 degrees depending on individual mobility. Partial squats typically reach 70–90 degrees. Knees should track in line with the toes throughout the movement — they should not cave inward (valgus) or drift excessively outward.

### Hip Flexion
At the bottom position, hip flexion typically reaches 100–120 degrees. The torso should maintain a forward lean of approximately 30–45 degrees from vertical, depending on bar placement (high bar vs. low bar). Excessive forward lean beyond 50 degrees may indicate limited ankle mobility or weak quadriceps.

### Ankle Dorsiflexion
Adequate ankle dorsiflexion of at least 15–20 degrees is needed for a full squat. Limited ankle mobility forces compensations such as excessive forward lean or heel rise.

### Spine Angle
The spine should remain neutral throughout the movement — no excessive rounding (kyphosis) of the thoracic or lumbar spine. A slight natural lordotic curve in the lumbar spine is expected and desirable.

## Common Form Faults

### Knee Valgus (Knees Caving In)
The knees collapse inward during the descent or ascent. This is often caused by weak hip abductors (gluteus medius) or poor motor control. It increases ACL injury risk and reduces force production. Coaching cue: "Push your knees out over your pinky toes."

### Excessive Forward Lean
The torso tilts too far forward, shifting the load onto the lower back. Often caused by limited ankle dorsiflexion, tight hip flexors, or weak quadriceps. Coaching cue: "Chest up, drive your back into the bar."

### Butt Wink (Posterior Pelvic Tilt)
The pelvis tucks under at the bottom of the squat, causing lumbar flexion. This can be due to limited hip flexion mobility or hamstring tightness. Coaching cue: "Stop just before you feel your hips tuck — that's your current depth."

### Heel Rise
The heels lift off the ground during descent, indicating limited ankle dorsiflexion. Coaching cue: "Keep your weight on your whole foot — feel the ground through your heels."

### Uneven Weight Distribution
Shifting more weight to one side, often visible as asymmetric hip or shoulder position. Can indicate a strength imbalance or previous injury compensation. Coaching cue: "Push evenly through both feet."

## Coaching Cues for Good Form
- "Brace your core — take a deep breath and push your abs out against your belt"
- "Break at the hips and knees simultaneously"
- "Sit back like you're sitting into a chair"
- "Drive through your midfoot on the way up"
- "Keep your chest proud and eyes forward"
- "Screw your feet into the floor" (external rotation cue for glute activation)

## Safety Considerations
Beginners should start with bodyweight squats or goblet squats before loading a barbell. Proper warm-up including hip circles, ankle mobility work, and light sets is essential. A squat rack with safety pins should always be used when training heavy.
