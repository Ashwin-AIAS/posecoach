# Conventional Deadlift — Form Guide

## Exercise Overview
The conventional deadlift is a compound hip-hinge movement that trains the entire posterior chain. It involves lifting a barbell from the floor to a standing lockout position. It is one of the most effective exercises for building total-body strength and is a standard powerlifting competition lift.

## Target Muscles
Primary: gluteus maximus, hamstrings (biceps femoris, semitendinosus, semimembranosus), erector spinae.
Secondary: quadriceps (during initial pull off floor), trapezius, rhomboids, latissimus dorsi (isometric), forearm flexors (grip), core stabilizers.

## Joint Actions
- Hip: extension throughout the lift
- Knee: extension during initial pull, then maintained near-straight
- Ankle: plantarflexion (slight, during lockout)
- Spine: maintained in neutral (no flexion or extension under load)

## Key Joint Angles — Correct Form

### Hip Angle
At the start position, hip flexion is approximately 65–80 degrees depending on limb proportions. At lockout, hips should reach full extension (approximately 170–180 degrees) with a slight posterior pelvic tilt to engage the glutes. The hip hinge is the primary movement driver.

### Knee Angle
At the start, knees are flexed to approximately 130–150 degrees (much less than a squat). During the pull, the knees straighten early in the lift. At lockout, knees should be fully extended but not hyperextended.

### Spine Angle
The spine must remain neutral throughout the entire lift. The torso angle at the start is typically 30–45 degrees from horizontal, depending on limb proportions. Any rounding of the lumbar spine under load is a serious form fault and injury risk.

### Shoulder Position
Shoulders should be directly over or slightly in front of the bar at the start. The scapulae should be retracted and depressed (pulled back and down). Arms remain straight throughout — they are hooks, not active pullers.

## Common Form Faults

### Lumbar Rounding (Flexion Under Load)
The lower back rounds during the pull, especially off the floor or during heavy attempts. This is the most dangerous deadlift fault — it places enormous shear force on the lumbar discs. Causes include pulling with a weight that is too heavy, poor bracing, or insufficient hamstring flexibility. Coaching cue: "Set your back before you pull — squeeze your chest up and push your belly between your thighs."

### Hips Rising Too Fast (Stiff-Leg Drift)
The hips shoot up before the bar leaves the floor, turning the deadlift into a stiff-legged deadlift. This shifts load to the lower back and removes the quadriceps from the initial pull. Coaching cue: "Push the floor away with your legs — don't just yank with your back."

### Bar Drifting Away From Body
The bar moves forward away from the shins during the pull, creating a longer moment arm and increasing lower back stress. The bar should travel in a straight vertical path, staying in contact with or very close to the shins and thighs. Coaching cue: "Drag the bar up your legs like you're painting your shins."

### Hyperextending at Lockout
Leaning back excessively at the top of the lift, often arching the lower back. This compresses the lumbar spine unnecessarily. Coaching cue: "Stand tall and squeeze your glutes — don't lean back."

### Jerking the Bar Off the Floor
Using a sudden yank to start the pull instead of building tension gradually. This can cause bicep tears (especially with mixed grip) and loss of spinal positioning. Coaching cue: "Take the slack out of the bar before you pull — squeeze it off the floor."

## Coaching Cues for Good Form
- "Wedge yourself into the bar — push your hips forward as you pull your chest up"
- "Take a deep breath, brace hard, then pull"
- "Push the floor away, don't pull the bar up"
- "Keep your lats tight — protect your armpits"
- "Lock out by driving your hips through, not by leaning back"
- "The bar stays on your body the whole way up"

## Safety Considerations
Beginners should learn the hip hinge pattern with Romanian deadlifts or kettlebell deadlifts before attempting heavy conventional pulls from the floor. Mixed grip can lead to bicep tears — hook grip or straps are safer alternatives for heavier loads. Always use bumper plates or raise the bar to standard pulling height.
