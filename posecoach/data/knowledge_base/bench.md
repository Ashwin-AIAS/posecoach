# Barbell Bench Press — Form Guide

## Exercise Overview
The barbell bench press is a compound upper-body pressing exercise performed lying supine on a flat bench. It is the primary exercise for developing chest strength and size, and is one of the three competitive powerlifting lifts.

## Target Muscles
Primary: pectoralis major (sternal and clavicular heads), anterior deltoid, triceps brachii.
Secondary: serratus anterior, coracobrachialis, core stabilizers (isometric).

## Joint Actions
- Shoulder: horizontal adduction and flexion during the press, horizontal abduction and extension during the descent
- Elbow: extension during the press, flexion during the descent
- Scapula: retraction and depression maintained throughout (stable base)

## Key Joint Angles — Correct Form

### Elbow Angle
At the bottom of the press, the elbow should reach approximately 90 degrees of flexion or slightly below (chest touch). At lockout, the elbows should be fully extended (approximately 170–180 degrees) but not hyperextended. The forearms should remain roughly vertical (perpendicular to the floor) throughout the movement when viewed from the side.

### Shoulder Abduction (Elbow Flare)
The angle between the upper arm and the torso should be approximately 45–75 degrees. Flaring the elbows out to 90 degrees (a "T" shape) places excessive stress on the shoulder joint and increases impingement risk. Tucking too far (below 30 degrees) shifts the exercise toward a close-grip press and reduces chest activation. The ideal angle balances shoulder safety with pectoral activation.

### Wrist Position
The wrist should remain stacked directly over the elbow when viewed from the front. The bar should sit on the heel of the palm, not high in the fingers. The wrist should not bend backward excessively.

### Spine and Scapula
A moderate arch in the thoracic spine is normal and safe — it helps retract the scapulae and creates a stable pressing platform. The shoulder blades should be squeezed together and pulled down toward the hips before unracking the bar. The lower back may have a slight natural arch but should not lift off the bench excessively.

## Common Form Faults

### Excessive Elbow Flare
The elbows splay out to 90 degrees from the torso, creating a "T" shape. This dramatically increases shoulder impingement risk, especially under heavy loads. Coaching cue: "Tuck your elbows to about 45 degrees — think of making an arrow shape, not a T."

### Losing Scapular Retraction
The shoulder blades lose their pinched position during the press, causing the shoulders to roll forward. This reduces pressing power and increases shoulder injury risk. Coaching cue: "Squeeze your shoulder blades together like you're holding a pencil between them — keep that squeeze the entire set."

### Bar Path Drift
The bar travels in a straight vertical line instead of the natural slight J-curve (bar moves slightly toward the face during the press). Or the bar drifts toward the belly. Coaching cue: "Press the bar up and slightly back toward your face."

### Bouncing Off Chest
Dropping the bar onto the chest and using the bounce to initiate the press. This removes the stretch reflex control, can crack ribs, and is an injury risk. Coaching cue: "Touch your chest gently — imagine there's a raw egg on your sternum."

### Feet Losing Contact
The feet lift off the floor during the press, reducing whole-body stability and leg drive. Coaching cue: "Drive your feet into the floor throughout the entire set."

## Coaching Cues for Good Form
- "Set up: eyes under the bar, squeeze your shoulder blades, arch your upper back"
- "Unrack with straight arms, bring the bar over your chest"
- "Lower to your lower chest / nipple line"
- "Press up and slightly back"
- "Keep your butt on the bench at all times"
- "Drive your feet into the ground for leg drive"

## Safety Considerations
Always bench press inside a power rack with safety pins, or use a spotter for heavy sets. Never use a thumbless ("suicide") grip — the bar can roll off the palms. Learn the roll of shame as a self-rescue technique. Warm up with lighter sets before working weight.
