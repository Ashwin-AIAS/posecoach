# Lunge — Form Guide

## Exercise Overview
The lunge is a unilateral compound lower-body exercise that trains each leg independently. It develops single-leg strength, balance, and coordination. Variations include forward lunges, reverse lunges, walking lunges, and split squats. The forward lunge is used as the default variation in PoseCoach.

## Target Muscles
Primary: quadriceps, gluteus maximus (front leg).
Secondary: hamstrings (front leg), hip adductors, calves (gastrocnemius, soleus), core stabilizers (for balance), gluteus medius (for lateral stability).

## Joint Actions (Front Leg)
- Hip: flexion during descent, extension during ascent
- Knee: flexion during descent, extension during ascent
- Ankle: dorsiflexion during descent

## Joint Actions (Back Leg)
- Hip: extension during descent (hip flexor stretch)
- Knee: flexion during descent
- Ankle: plantarflexion (on toes throughout)

## Key Joint Angles — Correct Form

### Front Knee Flexion
At the bottom of the lunge, the front knee should reach approximately 90 degrees of flexion. The knee should not travel significantly past the toes (though slight forward travel is normal and safe for healthy knees). The shin of the front leg should be close to vertical.

### Torso Position
The torso should remain vertical throughout the movement. A slight forward lean is acceptable but excessive lean indicates weak quadriceps or poor balance.

## Common Form Faults

### Front Knee Valgus (Collapsing Inward)
The front knee caves inward toward the midline during the lunge. This is the same fault as in squats — caused by weak hip abductors and poor motor control. It increases ACL and meniscus injury risk. Coaching cue: "Drive your front knee out over your pinky toe."

### Knee Shooting Far Past Toes
The front knee travels excessively forward past the toes, usually because the step length is too short. This increases patellofemoral joint stress. Coaching cue: "Take a longer step — your shin should stay close to vertical at the bottom."

### Torso Leaning Forward
The torso tilts forward significantly, shifting the load from the legs to the lower back. Coaching cue: "Stay tall — imagine a string pulling the crown of your head toward the ceiling."

### Losing Balance (Lateral Wobble)
Feet are placed in a line (like walking on a tightrope) rather than maintaining hip-width stance, causing lateral instability. Coaching cue: "Step forward on a train track, not a tightrope — keep your feet hip-width apart."

### Pushing Off Back Foot Instead of Front Foot
Using the back leg to push back up rather than driving through the front heel. This reduces the training stimulus on the target leg. Coaching cue: "Drive up through your front heel — your back leg is just for balance."

## Coaching Cues for Good Form
- "Take a comfortable step forward — not too short, not too long"
- "Lower your back knee straight down toward the floor"
- "Keep your torso upright and core braced"
- "Drive up through your front heel"
- "Keep your feet hip-width apart — two parallel tracks"
- "Control the descent — don't just drop down"

## Safety Considerations
Reverse lunges are generally easier to control than forward lunges and are recommended for beginners because the deceleration component is reduced. Lifters with knee pain should experiment with step length — a longer step shifts more load to the glutes and may be more comfortable. Start with bodyweight lunges before adding dumbbells or a barbell.
