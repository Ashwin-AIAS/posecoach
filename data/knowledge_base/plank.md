# Plank — Form Guide

## Exercise Overview
The plank is an isometric core stabilization exercise. Unlike dynamic exercises, the plank involves holding a static position for time. It trains anti-extension of the spine — the core's ability to resist forces that would pull the lumbar spine into excessive lordosis. It is foundational for core stability in all other exercises.

## Target Muscles
Primary: rectus abdominis (isometric), transverse abdominis, internal and external obliques.
Secondary: erector spinae (isometric co-contraction), gluteus maximus, quadriceps (isometric), anterior deltoid and serratus anterior (supporting the upper body).

## Joint Actions
There are no dynamic joint actions — the plank is an isometric hold. All joints are maintained in fixed positions.

## Key Joint Angles — Correct Form

### Spine Alignment
The spine should form a straight line from the head to the heels when viewed from the side. There should be no sagging (excessive lumbar extension) or piking (excessive hip flexion). The pelvis should be in a slight posterior tilt to engage the abdominals and flatten the lumbar curve.

### Hip Angle
The hip angle should be approximately 170–180 degrees (nearly straight). Any significant flexion at the hips (piking up) indicates the core is not maintaining the position and the hip flexors are compensating. Any significant extension (sagging) indicates the abdominals have fatigued and the load is being carried by the lumbar spine passively.

### Shoulder Position
The shoulders should be stacked directly over the elbows (forearm plank) or directly over the wrists (full plank). The shoulders should be depressed — not shrugged up toward the ears. The scapulae should be slightly protracted (pushed apart, not squeezed together).

### Neck Position
The neck should be in a neutral position as a natural extension of the spine. The gaze should be directed at the floor slightly ahead of the hands. Looking up or tucking the chin excessively both place the cervical spine out of alignment.

## Common Form Faults

### Hip Sagging (Excessive Lumbar Extension)
The hips drop toward the floor, creating an exaggerated arch in the lower back. This is the most common plank fault and indicates the core muscles have fatigued or were never properly engaged. It places compressive stress on the lumbar spine. Coaching cue: "Squeeze your glutes and tuck your hips slightly — imagine pulling your belt buckle toward your chin."

### Hip Piking (Excessive Hip Flexion)
The hips push up toward the ceiling, forming an inverted V shape. This makes the exercise easier by shortening the lever arm but removes the anti-extension training stimulus. Coaching cue: "Drop your hips until your body makes a straight line — have someone place a broomstick along your back to check."

### Shoulders Shrugging
The shoulders creep up toward the ears as fatigue sets in, tensing the upper trapezius and reducing scapular stability. Coaching cue: "Push your shoulders away from your ears — think of making your neck as long as possible."

### Head Dropping or Looking Up
The head drops toward the floor (chin tucked) or the person looks forward/up, both of which break spinal alignment. Coaching cue: "Look at the floor about a foot in front of your hands — your head is part of the straight line."

### Holding Breath
Many people unconsciously hold their breath during planks. This increases blood pressure and reduces endurance. Coaching cue: "Breathe normally — slow breaths in through your nose, out through your mouth."

## Coaching Cues for Good Form
- "Elbows directly under your shoulders"
- "Squeeze your glutes like you're holding a coin between them"
- "Tuck your pelvis slightly — flatten your lower back"
- "Push the floor away from you with your forearms"
- "Your body should be stiff as a board from head to heels"
- "Keep breathing — don't hold your breath"

## Progression and Duration
Beginners should aim for 20–30 seconds with perfect form, adding 5–10 seconds per week. Once 60 seconds with perfect form is achieved, progress to harder variations (weighted plank, long-lever plank, body saw) rather than simply holding longer. Planks held for multiple minutes with form breakdown provide minimal additional benefit and may cause lower back discomfort.

## Safety Considerations
Lifters with lower back pain should ensure they can maintain a neutral spine throughout the hold — reduce the duration if form breaks down rather than pushing through with a sagging back. Forearm planks are generally easier on the wrists than full planks and are preferred for most trainees.
