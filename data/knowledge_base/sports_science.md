# Sports Science Fundamentals

Broader principles that apply across gym training and most sports.

## Specificity and Adaptation

The body adapts to the specific demands you place on it. Train the qualities your
sport or goal needs — strength, speed, endurance, or skill — because adaptations
are largely specific to how you train. General fitness helps, but the closer the
training matches the target, the better it transfers.

## Energy Systems

Short, all-out efforts (under ~10 seconds) run on stored phosphocreatine.
High-intensity efforts up to a couple of minutes lean on glycolysis. Longer,
steady efforts are mostly aerobic. Most field sports mix all three, so well-
rounded conditioning trains each rather than only long slow cardio.

## Warm-Up and Performance

A good warm-up raises tissue temperature, primes the nervous system, and rehearses
the movement: a few minutes of general cardio, then specific ramp-up sets or
drills. Static stretching alone before power or strength work can briefly blunt
output, so save long holds for after training.

## Measuring Progress

Track objective markers — loads lifted, reps, times, bodyweight trends — rather
than relying on feel alone. Progress is rarely linear; expect plateaus and use
them as cues to adjust volume, intensity, or recovery rather than to quit.
