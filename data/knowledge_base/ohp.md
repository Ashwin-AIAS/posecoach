# Overhead Press (OHP) — Form Guide

## Exercise Overview
The standing barbell overhead press (also called the military press) is a compound upper-body exercise that involves pressing a barbell from shoulder height to full overhead lockout. It is one of the best exercises for developing shoulder strength and stability, and it heavily involves the core for stabilization.

## Target Muscles
Primary: anterior deltoid, lateral deltoid, triceps brachii.
Secondary: upper pectoralis major (clavicular head), trapezius (upper fibers), serratus anterior, core stabilizers (rectus abdominis, obliques, erector spinae — all working isometrically to prevent spinal extension).

## Joint Actions
- Shoulder: flexion and abduction during the press, extension and adduction during the descent
- Elbow: extension during the press, flexion during the descent
- Scapula: upward rotation during the press
- Spine: should remain neutral — no extension under load

## Key Joint Angles — Correct Form

### Shoulder Angle
At the start position, the upper arms are approximately parallel to the floor or slightly below (shoulder flexion around 85–95 degrees). At lockout, the arms should be fully overhead with the bar directly over the midfoot, ears, and shoulders in a straight vertical line (approximately 170–180 degrees of shoulder flexion).

### Spine Angle
The spine must remain neutral throughout the movement. Any excessive lumbar extension (leaning back) to complete the press is a form fault that compresses the lumbar spine. A very slight lean back to clear the chin is acceptable, but the torso should immediately return to vertical once the bar passes the head.

### Wrist Position
The wrist should be stacked directly under the bar with minimal backward bend. The bar rests on the heel of the palm.

## Common Form Faults

### Excessive Lumbar Extension (Leaning Back)
The lifter arches the lower back to press the weight overhead, effectively turning the overhead press into an incline press. This places dangerous compressive and shear forces on the lumbar spine. It typically indicates the weight is too heavy or the lifter has weak shoulder strength relative to chest strength. Coaching cue: "Squeeze your glutes and abs hard — they're your anti-lean-back muscles."

### Pressing in Front of the Body
The bar path moves forward rather than straight up, creating a longer moment arm. At lockout the bar ends up in front of the head rather than directly overhead. Coaching cue: "Move your head out of the way — push it through the window once the bar passes your forehead."

### Flared Elbows at the Start
The elbows point outward rather than slightly forward at the start position. This reduces pressing leverage and increases shoulder impingement risk. Coaching cue: "Point your elbows slightly forward and in at the start — like you're trying to show someone your armpits."

### Incomplete Lockout
Not fully extending the arms overhead, which leaves the load supported by muscle rather than the skeletal structure. Coaching cue: "Push the bar all the way up until your elbows lock — shrug your shoulders up slightly at the top."

## Coaching Cues for Good Form
- "Squeeze your glutes, tighten your quads — your legs are your foundation"
- "Big breath, brace your core like someone's going to punch you"
- "Bar starts on the front delts, not the collarbone"
- "Push your head through once the bar clears your forehead"
- "Lock it out directly over your ears"
- "Lower the bar under control back to your shoulders"

## Safety Considerations
The overhead press demands adequate shoulder mobility — lifters who cannot raise their arms fully overhead without arching their back should work on shoulder and thoracic mobility before loading the movement. Start with dumbbells or a lighter barbell. Never press behind the neck — this position places the shoulder in extreme external rotation under load and is an injury risk for most lifters.
