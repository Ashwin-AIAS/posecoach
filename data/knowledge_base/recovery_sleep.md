# Recovery and Sleep

Training is the stimulus; recovery is when you actually adapt.

## Sleep

Sleep is the highest-leverage recovery tool. Most adults need 7–9 hours. Poor
sleep lowers strength, raises perceived effort, impairs muscle gain, and increases
injury risk. Keep a consistent schedule, a cool dark room, and limit caffeine and
screens late. One bad night is fine; chronic deprivation quietly wrecks progress.

## Managing Fatigue

Fatigue accumulates across sessions. Signs you are under-recovered include stalled
lifts, low motivation, poor sleep, and lingering soreness. The fix is usually a
lighter deload week, more sleep, or more food — not pushing harder. Stress from
outside the gym counts too.

## Active Recovery and Mobility

Light movement on off days — walking, easy cycling, mobility drills — can aid
recovery and joint health without adding training stress. Stretch or mobilise the
areas that feel tight, but mobility work supplements training; it does not replace
progressive overload.

## Consistency Over Perfection

Recovery is unglamorous: sleep, food, gentle movement, and not doing too much.
Nailing these basics most of the time outperforms any supplement, gadget, or
recovery hack marketed as a shortcut.
