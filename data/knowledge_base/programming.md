# Program Design

How to structure training for strength, muscle, and conditioning.

## Progressive Overload

The core driver of progress is doing slightly more over time — more weight, more
reps, more quality sets, or better technique. Pick one or two lifts to push and
add a little each week or two. Without progression, a program stalls regardless of
how clever it looks.

## Hypertrophy (Muscle Growth)

For muscle, accumulate volume: roughly 10–20 hard sets per muscle group per week,
mostly in the 5–30 rep range, taken close to failure (1–3 reps in reserve).
Train each muscle about twice a week. Manage fatigue so quality stays high across
sets.

## Strength

For maximal strength, emphasise heavier loads (about 1–6 reps) on the main lifts
with longer rest (2–5 minutes), and keep total hard sets moderate. Practise the
specific lifts you want to get strong at. Strength is a skill as much as a
capacity.

## Conditioning and Recovery

Add 1–3 conditioning sessions a week (intervals or steady state) without
sabotaging lifting recovery. Deload every 4–8 weeks — a lighter week — when
performance or motivation dips. A simple full-body or upper/lower split run
consistently beats a complex split done sporadically.
