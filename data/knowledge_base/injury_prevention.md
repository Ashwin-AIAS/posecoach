# Injury Prevention and General Rehab

General educational information on training around and reducing injury risk. This
is not medical advice — see a qualified professional for diagnosis or a rehab
plan.

## Reducing Injury Risk

Most gym injuries come from doing too much too soon, poor technique under fatigue,
or skipping preparation. Progress load gradually (roughly 5–10% per week is a
common guideline), warm up the working joints, and stop a set when form breaks
down rather than grinding ugly reps. Sleep and recovery matter as much as the
training itself, because tissue adapts between sessions, not during them.

## Common Lifting Niggles

Lower-back tightness after deadlifts or squats often traces back to losing a
neutral spine under load or bracing poorly. Shoulder pain in pressing usually
links to flaring the elbows or pressing through a painful range. Knee aches in
squats and lunges are frequently a load or volume spike rather than a single
"bad" movement. Reducing range to a pain-free zone, lowering load, and improving
control usually settles a minor niggle.

## Pain vs Soreness

Delayed onset muscle soreness is dull, symmetrical, and fades over a few days —
it is normal. Sharp, joint-centred, or one-sided pain that lingers or worsens is
a signal to back off and, if it persists, get it assessed. Never train through
sharp joint pain.

## When To See a Professional

Seek a physiotherapist or doctor for pain that lasts more than a couple of weeks,
numbness or tingling, a joint that gives way or locks, or any pain following a
sudden "pop". Rehab should be individualised — generic internet protocols are a
starting point for questions, not a substitute for assessment.
