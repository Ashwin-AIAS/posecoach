# Bicep Curl — Form Guide

## Exercise Overview
The bicep curl is an isolation exercise that targets the elbow flexors. It can be performed with a barbell, dumbbells, or an EZ-bar. While simpler than compound lifts, proper form is important for maximizing muscle activation and preventing shoulder or wrist injuries.

## Target Muscles
Primary: biceps brachii (long head and short head), brachialis.
Secondary: brachioradialis (forearm), anterior deltoid (if swinging occurs — this is a fault, not a feature), forearm flexors.

## Joint Actions
- Elbow: flexion during concentric (lifting), extension during eccentric (lowering)
- Shoulder: should remain stationary — any movement is a form fault
- Wrist: should remain neutral — no excessive flexion or extension

## Key Joint Angles — Correct Form

### Elbow Flexion
At the bottom position, the elbow should be near full extension (approximately 160–170 degrees) but not locked out or hyperextended. At the top of the curl, elbow flexion reaches approximately 30–50 degrees. The full range of motion is critical for complete muscle development.

### Shoulder Position
The upper arm should remain perpendicular to the floor and close to the torso throughout the movement. The shoulder angle should stay at approximately 0–10 degrees of flexion. Any forward swing of the shoulder indicates momentum is being used instead of muscle contraction.

### Wrist Position
The wrist should remain in a neutral position throughout the movement. Excessive wrist flexion (curling the wrist inward at the top) or extension places unnecessary stress on the wrist joint and reduces bicep activation.

## Common Form Faults

### Swinging / Using Momentum
The torso swings backward to help lift the weight, using momentum rather than bicep strength. This is the most common curl fault and indicates the weight is too heavy. It reduces muscle activation and increases lower back stress. Coaching cue: "Pin your elbows to your sides and keep your torso completely still."

### Incomplete Range of Motion
Not fully extending the elbow at the bottom or not fully flexing at the top. Partial reps reduce the stretch and contraction stimulus. Coaching cue: "All the way down, all the way up — full range every rep."

### Elbow Drift Forward
The elbows move forward in front of the body during the curl, which shifts tension from the biceps to the anterior deltoid. Coaching cue: "Keep your elbows pinned — they don't move, only your forearms do."

### Excessive Wrist Curl
Flexing the wrist at the top of the movement to "squeeze" the rep. This loads the wrist joint without adding meaningful bicep stimulus. Coaching cue: "Keep your wrist straight — imagine your hand is a hook."

## Coaching Cues for Good Form
- "Stand tall with your shoulders back and down"
- "Elbows glued to your ribs"
- "Squeeze the bicep at the top, then lower under control"
- "2 seconds up, 3 seconds down" (tempo cue for control)
- "Don't swing — if you have to swing, the weight is too heavy"

## Safety Considerations
Start with a weight that allows 10–12 controlled reps without any body swing. Avoid locking out the elbows aggressively at the bottom to protect the joint. If using a straight barbell, switch to an EZ-bar if wrist pain occurs — the angled grip reduces wrist strain.
